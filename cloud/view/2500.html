14 October 2009

Young peoples digital cultures, Keri Facer

What are they doing? Study - "The learner and their context", BECTA 2008-2011, Oxford, John Furlong

At home: gaming, social networking, looking up information, different patterns of use by age – 4 different groups – active participation, all-rounders, normative, peripheral > learning to use digital tools from peers, from cultural artefacts, from conversations

In School: office basics taken for granted by end of primary school, in exceptional cases some use of twitter and social networking (usually clamped down), school introduces mathematics functional tools, interactive whiteboards, VLEs and e-portfolios, audit and performances tools, mobile devices increasingly used (with limited pedagogical understanding?)

Implications? Dual identities…

Schizophrenic educational experience of an 18-year old in 2015

Born 1997 – standards, targets and centralised control

Primary school: SureStart programme, literacy and numeracy strategies, prescribed personalised, in later years SEAL (Social and Emotional Learning), hot-housing/tutoring (esp. London/Kent/Grammar regions)

Secondary school: failure of centralised command/control; failure of standards approach. Freeing up curriculum. Curriculum aims, competencies and learning to learn, student voice/distributed leadership, extended schools, area-based partnerships, creative partnerships, every child matters extended services, compulsory attendance to 18

Also the rise of alternative educational experiences – 5min.com, 12 sec.com and YouTube, online schools, the school of everything, UK rise in homes schooling and alternative modes of schooling, Steiner education

Socio-technical developments?

From the Horizon programme

  • Demographic changes: over 50% of population ages 50 by 2030 with a further 40 years life expectance
  • Working and living alongside machines becomes increasingly normal – devolving responsibility to machines, simple manual tasks or outsourcings the management of complex systems, ethical tensions and public debate, different generations with different degrees of comfort in delegating power and responsibility to machines
  • We will have the capacity to know more stuff, to remember more
  • The personal cloud – capacity to connect to a network and constantly connected to knowledge, resources, people and tools
  • Distance matters less, geography still counts

What are the roles of universities and colleges in interesting times?

  • What sort of relationships between people might university’s and colleges foster? What will our new roes and responsibilities be?
  • What sorts of knowledge and understanding might learners in these different contexts need?
  • What curriculum works for – mixed demographics, networked, mobile learners, operating in information rich environments and preparing for highly polarised workspaces?
