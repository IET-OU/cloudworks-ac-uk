David White
8:41am 17 March 2011

 Hello, myself and Melissa Highton will be talking about our OER Impact project for JISC and OER activity in general around the University of Oxford. The Impact project (http://www.jisc.ac.uk/whatwedo/programmes/elearning/oer2/oerimpact.aspx) is investigating the academic use/reuse of OER.

I'm very interested in stories of the use/reuse of OER and how they fit in the broad spectrum of online resource use. To help position the conversations we are having with people we have developed a 'map' of use/reuse: http://oerblog.conted.ox.ac.uk/?p=1 which you might find helpful?

Our workshop will be looking at the use/reuse side of the OER coin and at what it means form an institution to be 'open'. Where does openness take pace around your institution and what form does it take?

Hello David,

as you might have gathered, I'm quite new to OER, and I'm familiar with language teaching more than anything else, but seeing your map, I was wondering whether it would be sensible (or silly!) to make a distinction between an Educationnal Resource (what you use when teaching / learning), and the content it is built around / upon. An example might be clearer : as a language teacher, I can use songs, videos, images, anything to design an activity (the "material" would be what I called the content, and the activity would be the resource). Other teachers might reuse the material in a completely different activity... I'm not sure I would say I adapt the OER in this case - unless you consider any document you use in a classroom is an OER (which can be an option as well).

I hope my point is clear, but if it isn't or doesn't fit the questions or discussion you'd like to raise, please feel free to say so (I'm not sure this is relevant for a discussion about what it means for an institution to be open...).

Comment 3 by David White

@Sandrine The distinction between 'content' and 'activity design' in the context of OER is complex. My view is that something only becomes OER when it is used for education, up to that point it is simply CC licensed 'stuff'. Some people use the Open CourseWare (OCW) title to make the distinction between OER that contains a learning design/structure and those that are standalone chunks of content like images.

@Tita I'm a huge fan of mapping concepts onto a quadrant of thematic areas. When this type of exercise is discussed it brings useful narrative to the fore.

Comment 5 by David White

David White
4:13pm 21 March 2011 Permalink


I suppose my point is that you can use almost anything as part of an educational process. It could be a single image or the structure of a whole module. It has the potential to be OER if it's CC licensed but it only becomes OER when it's used educationally (to teach or learn). This was we don't make assumptions about how it might be appropriated. 

The map is in quadrants to help roughly define use/reuse areas. The reality is the use/reuse takes place one a sliding scale across the whole of that area. The map would work without the quadrant but becomes difficult to reference.
