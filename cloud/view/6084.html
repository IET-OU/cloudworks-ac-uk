Ethics, equity and excellence: a case study of supported transition to higher education in the Australian context

Tamra Cocks and Jennifer Stokes

Australia’s higher education (HE) widening participation targets aim to develop the nation as a knowledge economy. This overview paper will consider tensions arising between policy and practice, capturing some of the challenges inherent in balancing the twin agendas of equity and excellence, through a case study of Foundation Studies at an Australian university college.

 

Extra content

Interview with the authors

I’m interested to learn why you chose to pursue this piece of research: Why these issues in particular?

We are interested in exploring ethics, equity and excellence as these are issues which present themselves on a daily basis in widening participation education. We want to explore how to manage tensions in the balance between equitable access to higher education and maintaining the university’s expectations of “excellence”. Widening participation policies have been created in the national interest, however implementing the policies creates certain challenges. Our research reflects upon the ethics of bringing a diverse student cohort into a system which was designed for the ‘elite’ and hopes to offer insight into how this might be better managed.  This provides a case study of putting policy into practice and the ethical challenges this presents for the university teacher at the “coalface”.

Thanks Tamra, that’s an excellent introduction. In the UK, throughout the last fifteen years there has been a high level and concerted policy effort to widen participation to those under-represented in HE. Alongside this debate there has consistently raged an argument which has on the one hand the cynics who suggest that to widen participation necessarily requires or leads to a ‘dumbing down’ of the academy; and on the other, those that maintain a strong ideological commitment to the socially just outcome of fair opportunity and access. Do these or similar arguments appear in Australian policy and media fora?

Yes this debate certainly exists in the Australian context. In recent years we’ve had a government with a strong bias towards educational access. This has resulted in clear equity targets for universities which has received mixed responses from the media. While more progressive institutions have supported wider participation targets, the media has enjoyed reporting the perspective of traditional institutions which have expressed some reticence about potential impacts on graduate quality. A traditional view suggests that students who do not receive university offers are excluded because of their lack of commitment and/or suitability for a higher education degree program. However there is growing recognition that it is external factors rather than intellectual capabilities that determines access to university.

Where do you see this writing and yourself heading in the future? Are there particular themes or strands of the work that you particularly want to follow up on?

We’re interested in discussing these ideas at the conference and hoping this will inform a publication. In future research we hope to collect data in order to ascertain how universities can better support students from equity groups in achieving tertiary qualifications.
