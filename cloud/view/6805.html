Project continued as solo effort

Your situation (context)

I want to produce a straightforward introduction to the collaborative use of virtual worlds aimed at  postgraduate students (science in my case). The environment should be simple, modular and amenable to modification at a basic level by the students working individually and as groups. There should be a generic activity that students undertake to highlight the benefits and current limitations of the environment. A separate area will speculate on the future of such technology. Development has started: see http://tidalblog.blogspot.co.uk/search/label/oldsmooc

The change you would like to see (challenge)

What do you hope to achieve?

Produce an environment that will facilitate collaborative projects both globally and locally and students who are more open to new approaches to working.

What would be different if you succeed?

Informed decisions might lead to use of virtual worlds for some types of project, insight into the nature of collaborative working and a reduction in unnecessary travel and hence cost and carbon footprint.

How you might go about bringing that change?

What is your pedagogical approach?

Under consideration.

Which technologies will you use to implement it...

OpenSim. This is an actively developed open source environment  for which there are multiple hosting solutions and the ability to backup the environment, run it locally from a USB stick in offline mode and network in a secure fashion both locally in the form of grids and globally via a technology known as the Hypergrid.

and how?

Project development is being undertaken by myself on a best-endeavours basis using a solution hosted on the commercial Kitely cloud-based grid. If the final version is deemed of sufficient interest or value, it may be released as an OER.

Comment 4 by Peter Miller

I'm still pondering the nature of the collaborative task. It has to be in some sense generic and capable of being broken down into sub-tasks. One possibility would be to model something approximating a Sherlock Holmes story involving multiple crime scenes, have pairs of students investigate each scene and then compile the evidence and collectively see if they can identify the miscreant. I should add that the mystery element here is completely secondary to illustrating use of the environment. It doesn't matter if the story is familiar or the students read it online as they go. The aim is to give them a focus.
