Comment 3 by Will Woods

Will Woods
1:37pm 7 October 2009 Permalink


I asked about paper versus interactive table because this interests me. Children enjoy paper and "tangable" objects and work with them differently than with online equivalents. I think in the tabletop experiment the table added something to the way things were negotiated, because as Paul pointed out there was less opportunity to 'grab' all the pieces in the way you could do with paper so it forced negotiation. I like that as it adds value to the experience. As Jon Rosewell pointed out a wiki equivalent of a table would be great to really stimulate the 'pulling apart and rebuilding' needed for ideas forming and for reuse, so for OER's for example. Food for thought.

I'm actually quite interested in tabletops for playing board games as lots of board games are quite time-consuming to set up and to manage in terms of tracking scores etc. I'd be quite curious as to how it's difference playing a game normally vs. playing the same game but on an interactive tabletop.

I was wondering too about language- most higher-level learning is usually very language-based. Working jointly on text with two people round a computer often works really well - better than sending a doc backwards and forwards, but doesn't scale to more than that really.
