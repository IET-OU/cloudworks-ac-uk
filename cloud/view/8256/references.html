Title

Introduction to Endnote

Narrator

I am the workshop facilitator.

Situation

The workshop took place in our library PC classroom, tucked away on the top floor of the building. It seats 30 participants in five rows of six PCs, all facing forward towards a whiteboard and the instructors PC. The course, took place in the morning, timetabled for 9:30 – 12:30. The room is equipped with a radio microphone, induction loops and the instructor has the facility to capture the screens of the other computers, for purposes of demonstration.

Eleven postgraduate researchers attended the session, from different departments, most of whom did not know each other before the workshop. They had mainly come at the suggestion of either peers who had been on the course before, or their research supervisors, to learn how to use reference management software for their research.

Task

In this course I am trying to give the participants experience and confidence in using the Endnote reference management software, contextualised for academic research. Success is measured partly by the reaction of the participants on the day, partly via feedback forms (‘happy sheets’) at the end of the workshop and partly by on-going contact with the participants.

Actions

  1. Welcome and Introduction: Introduction of myself, explanation of the format of the workshop and the importance of referencing and reference management.
  2. Downloading from the university library catalogue: Download references directly from the library catalogue to Endnote, creating a new reference and the importance of reference type, downloading from a library catalogue search to Endnote. Some of the computers cannot have their screens captured from the front; ask the students to watch another screen or the front whiteboard. Accommodate late comers.
  3. Hands-on time: Distribute the manuals, wander around answering questions.
  4. Endnote and Word: Describe (in a story) the reasons for having accurate referencing in a thesis or journal article. Demonstrate creating references in a Word document.
  5. Hands-on time: Wander around answering questions.
  6. Endnote styles: Describe the styles available in Endnote and show how to do very simple modifications to them. Quite a lot of students get lost at this point; reassure them that it is not essential to understand the details, but they need to know how styles work in general.
  7. Hands-on time: Wander around answering questions.
  8. Break: Phew. Distribute link to the online feedback form.
  9. Downloading references from on-line databases: Demonstrate downloading article references from on-line databases, starting with Web of Knowledge, JSTOR and then Google Scholar. Ask for other “favourite databases” to demonstrate; this time I don’t get any. Show how to attach the full text of an article to a reference. JSTOR is slow so I revert to an article already saved.
  10. Hands-on time: Wander around answering questions.
  11. Wrap up: Just before participants start to leave, wrap up and remind them about the blog, sending questions to the IT Service Desk and to fill in the feedback form. Answer any final questions.

Results

In the feedback, 62% of respondents answered that they would use the software, frequently, while the rest said they would use it occasionally. All respondents answered that the course met their expectations; most regarded the length and level of the course as being just right.

Reflections

This is a course I have taught many times, in various formats. It is always useful to interact with students from different backgrounds and different subject areas, and to hear the questions, which might indicate my lack of delivering a good learning experience, or not covering a full range of material. One feedback comment referred to inadequate information on Endnote Groups, which are covered in another course (‘Advanced Endnote’); it is possible that some exchange of material between that course and this one could help.

Overall, however, the course seems well honed and fit for purpose.
