Katherine's Design Narrative: Introducing Wikis to CAE

  • Favourite
  • 612 views

Cloud created by:

Katherine Anderton
7 April 2015

Title Using Wikis as a teaching tool in a CAE exam training class

Narrator

Teacher of 18 adult learners in a 2-hour evening class

Situation

Within the University where I work, we offer CAE training courses to adult learners.  The learners range in age from 18 to 65+.  Some are also following a degree course at the university, others are generally working professionals who wish to improve their English for personal or private reasons.  For this latter group, they may not have followed any formal training or education program for 20 years or more.  As the class is in the late evening, the students are sometimes tired having been at work all day.  In addition, they may not have established learning routines.  

Task

It was noticed that a lot of the students were not making any or only a few effective (or sometimes correct) notes.  As the text book is quite busy and difficult to navigate, extra information is often given via other media in the class from which the students need to take notes.  In addition, it was observed that students sometimes 'switched off'  in class, if they didn't have an active role to play.

Actions

The students were given the responsibility for their own collective notes.  Two students are named each week to take the notes for the whole class.  They then had to post their notes within a group wiki on Blackboard.  Two students were chosen in order that their combined notes covered everything discussed in the class.  Rather than relying on one student to minute the classroom.  Other students were allowed to edit the wiki if they spotted anything missing or wrong.  The teacher informed the group she would not be correcting the wiki as if it were a writing assignment, unless there was something glaringly wrong or that might mislead other students.

The students were allowed to add external material to the notes.   E.g. websites with useful explanations, exercises or articles; images and diagrams; or videos they found amusing or that illustrated a topic.

Results

This is still in progress so final results cannot be seen, however, overall the students are performing the basic task of creating a wiki of class notes for each week fairly effectively.  By having two students work together, the 'responsibility' to produce the notes was shared, but it also encouraged them to do the task to avoid letting down the other.

However the students do not seem to have read the notes - probably planning on coming back to them before the final test at the end of the course.  In addition, whilst there have been a few small but simple to correct errors showing up on the wiki - none of the students have stepped up to do so.  As stated previously, the teacher's role was not as a corrector of English, but as a form of 'caretaker' only if and when necessary.

The quality of the wiki was not always high - sometimes the students had just typed out the few notes they had made in class, without adding any extra supporting material that would make the wiki more interesting.

Reflections

The use of the wiki was interesting as it allowed the teacher to see what students had considered note-worthy from the class.  For example, little cultural discussions that came up in the lesson were sometimes described in great deal on the wiki.  As stated in the results, sometimes the quality of the wikis varied.

When doing this again, I would be more specific in what is expected in terms of effort, quality and content.  Possibly by showing a sample wiki page done well.

It might also be a good idea to have a little quiz each week based on the notes of the wiki.  The students who write the wiki entry could also write a little 5-question test.

The wiki could also potentially be 'flipped'.  By giving two students 2 or 3 areas we are going to cover in the next class and allow them to research it and provide an explanation, exercises and links in a wiki that can then be corrected/added to after the class.

On a personal reflection, it was also interesting to see where my own diviation from the lesson plan (often in response to a question or comment) may have caused confusion or a lack of continuity in the classroom.
