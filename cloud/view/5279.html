Design-Practice Activity 3 - 'Representing Designs'

  • Favourite
  • 1228 views

Cloud created by:

Paul Mundin
5 April 2011

This activity will take about an hour.

  • Read the Resource (Conole, 2009) 'An overview of design representations'

This document builds on the Resource 'The Design Lifecycle' and looks at the ways a learning design initiated during the 'Visioning' stage and then iteratively developed during the 'Gathering' and 'Assembling' stages could be represented, either textually or visually.

  • Do you create and/or use any learning activity representations textually or visually?
  • Would you find find designing a learning activity using a visual representation helpful?
  • What are the advantages or disadvantages of creating and using visual representations?
  • What tool(s) do you use to create a design representation?

You could:

  • Post/share your thoughts about the above questions or the document in this Cloud
  • If you have one - embed one of your own visual representations with a brief description - for sharing with the other participants
  • Spend some time reading and responding to the postings of other participants
  • Respond to postings about your comments

As I have said above - to me it seems a bit of a step back, jumping through hoops and i don't think particularly helpful for me anyway.

I'd love to hear other people who do and would find it useful.

Do you create and/or use any learning activity representations textually or visually? In my development of face-to-face tutorials I make use of both textual and visual support materials

Would you find find designing a learning activity using a visual representation helpful?   yes and for this purpose I make use of diagrams, flow charts, mind map simulations

What are the advantages or disadvantages of creating and using visual representations?

advantages include: providing an extended/alternative form of representing concepts and principles; many times my use of graphics allows for opportunities for students to actively participate in the develop of the visual representation and so becomes a collaborative activity between myself as the AL (more experienced other) and the student participants based on Lev Vygotsky’s concept of zone of proximal development  (ZPD), Barbara Rogoff’s concept of collaborative practice and scaffolding learning  and Jerome Bruner’s  concept of scaffolding

disadvantages: include additional time in the preparation of face-to-face tutorials; the type of representation is dependent on the specific concept or principle that it is my intention to communicate;  limited to my knowledge and expertise of the resources and technological tools available

What tool(s) do you use to create a design representation?   PowerPoint, Microsoft Excel; Microsoft Word; pen and paper; flip charts

I add:  that in relation to the “different types of representation” included in the chart presented on page four of the embedded document, I consider that of the seven identified representations, my involvement as an AL teaching on a Level 1 undergraduate module, I implement three of the seven: (3) Task swimline representation; (5) Design method: the pedagogy profile, I note that I would add to the “experience and activity” point on the pyramid, written assignment; (7) Course map, within the structure of the “Course map” identifying five elements, I consider that my contribution as an AL is concentrated within the sphere of facilitating, supporting and guiding students within the context of the module overall design and learning intentions leading to a Foundation Degree in Primary Teaching and Learning, which encompasses a suite of three individual modules (120 undergraduate credits)

Comment 10 by Liz Middleton

Liz Middleton
8:17pm 9 June 2011 Permalink


  • Do you create and/or use any learning activity representations textually or visually?

I feel as if I am more of a visual learner myself and find flow charts and min maps helpful. I have experimented with different visual formats for preparing and planning my learning activities-I have been using a mind mapping process using ‘Inspirations’ to create the Elluminate session I ran with students this evening.

  • Would you find designing a learning activity using a visual representation helpful?

Potentially would like to explore the use of other formats but once the language used gets a little technical and there is the need to go to various websites and download the tools needed I do start to panic!

  • What are the advantages or disadvantages of creating and using visual representations?

The advantages are that is does help to structure the thinking for me as my ideas can flow all over the place otherwise-it consolidates issues and really get me to look at process as well as content,

What struck me this evening for example was realizing that in order to make sure the Elluminate session went well I had to keep the design as simple as possible as it was to be a new learning experience for me as well as some of the students involved. I also realized once I was in full flow of the session that there was so much to keep focused on-which hands were up to speak, what comments were written in the comment box  by students, how to keep ensuring that they were all okay with the set up as well as the content-perhaps a better ‘map’ of the process for me as well would have been helpful to create??

It will be interesting to see what feedback there is from students especially those who have dyslexia or  other issues that affect more direct  ‘text’ based learning

Disadvantages

Definitely the time it takes all round-becoming familiar with the tools to use, for example in the first place-presumably (I keep telling myself) once that happens there is less time to put aside for the process

What tool(s) do you use to create a design representation?

Those in Inspirations and other mind mapping software mostly plus symbols and flow charts I have created on paper.
