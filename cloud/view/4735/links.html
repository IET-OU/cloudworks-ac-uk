Keynote: Raquel Xalabarder: Copyright laws and OER

  • Favourite
  • 1247 views

Cloud created by:

Patrick McAndrew
2 November 2010

Raquel Xalabarder

Raquel’s theme is copyright – defined as a way to encourage innovation. Her approach is to list all the things that we should know by saying “I am not going to tell you…” about the impact of laws, limitations, risks of non-copyright uses,

Interest of authors is not the only aspect – general rights and public interest can take priority and particularly in the academic world awareness is needed of “statutorily exempted acts” which allow fair use. The uncertainty around this does mean that playing safe may mean licences are bought.

However domestic laws are doing a poor job, international norms at EU levels attention is paid to educational use. Allowing educational and research use – that is then not reflected in the national laws (which often reflect lobbying).

WIPO – comparative study of domestic laws shows the need for much work to pay attention to the law on educational use.

Instead licencing is used to work with copyrighted material – but it does not solve the fundamental issues of the law and the material that is not explicitly licenced. Open licencing will only partly address.

“Copyright maybe difficult but it has some good in it.” It can foster creativity and only if we have sensible laws that recognise educational needs to avoid the trap of ending up in a licenced world rather than a legislated world.

Prof Lessig: Copyright should cover something less than it all – deal with what it needs to. The licenced commons does not equate to the public domain and so a broad exemption for education and sensibly limited terms will free up an give benefits to society alongside those of the author. The benefits of education are very apparent and can provide a good argument for change.

One of the first copyright laws – 1710. Required 9 copies of the printed books would be deposited with the (9) existing Universities of the United Kingdom. At that time copyright law was there to ensure that education could make use of the output of the authors.

Educational rights are for everyone and this is recognised at international level, author rights though dominate the domestic laws. An opportunity lies in the recognised need for a different approach for developing countries in the annex of the Berne convention – but not fully adopted. WIPO (World Intellectual Property Organisation) also considers the ways that IP can be sensibly enforced across the world. This is not best done though through bi-lateral agreements that impose the less than perfect copyright laws and harsh view of IP on other countries. Anti-Counterfeiting Trade Agreement (ACTA) takes a territorial and enforcing approach that in attempting to replace fora with enforcement – but that needs good laws that are not there at the moment.

Access to culture and education in the law needs to enshrined in the law – so that copyright is no longer seen as a “No entry” sign but instead allows use for the benefit of society.

 

Qs: US Patent law allows inventions to be declared public is that a good model?

A: That works in US law- but in the EU this is not possible and cannot be given away in a similar way.

Q: What is the right amount of time?

A: There should be variation – e.g. teaching material is additional to the main activity. Life plus 70 years is too long.

Q: Universities are producers as well as consumers

A: True but need to recognise the use rights more strongly
