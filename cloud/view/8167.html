Aims and Objectives of the session:

  • To consider the purpose and value of the Probationary Assessment
  • To consider ways to prepare for the Probationary Assessment
  • To consider how to move on from the Probationary Assessment

The session will cover:

  • The place of the Probationary Assessment in the PhD
  • The individual elements of the Probationary Assessment
  • The student and supervisor experience

1. The probation report – due at the end of month 9 for full-time students, and at the end of month 18 for part-time students – amounts to a research proposal and must contain the following key ingredients:

· A feasible research question

· A review of existing research

· A research approach, including justification for that approach

· A work schedule or plan with timeline covering the whole of the remaining research programme, including any interim deadlines, and indicating task dependencies and contingency planning.

The length and format of the probation report can vary, depending on disciplinary requirements and conventions.

2. The mini-viva

The probation mini-viva – which should be completed by the end of month 10 for full-time students, and at the end of month 20 for part-time students – is conducted by at least two probation assessors: experienced research academics who are not the student’s supervisors. The form of the mini-viva may vary across the university: some disciplines structure it along the lines of a PhD defense, others have review panels. Sometimes the supervisors participate actively; sometimes they attend as observers. Whatever the form, the focus of the discussion is on the student’s research skills and progress so far, as reflected by the probation report.

3. The oral presentation

Students must present their work orally to a semi-public audience, such as a department seminar, student colloquium, or workshop. This should be completed by the end of month 10 for full-time students, and the end of month 20 for part-time students. The purpose is to start students presenting their work early, both to develop their presentation skills and to encourage them to expose their work to scrutiny and discussion regularly. The requirement is simply for the student to have given a research talk, not to have given it to any particular standard. Just doing it is sufficient. Therefore, the supervisor’s formal role in the assessment is simply to document that the talk has been given, and the probation assessors simply check that this has been recorded.

4. The summary of PhD skills development

As part of their probation assessment, students must provide a summary of their PhD skills development activities to date. The summary is a synopsis of the state of the Progress File at probation and should encompass an updated skills audit and needs analysis, an associated development plan, and evidence of research skills and development activities in the probationary period.

The Progress File is an ongoing portfolio of PhD skills development which students must maintain throughout their PhD studies. The Progress File is a record of existing and developing skills, as demonstrated through specific evidence. As the student progresses through the degree, the three elements – needs analysis, development plan, and evidence of research skills and development – should be updated regularly, and the progress file should accumulate evidence of addressing the needs identified along the way and hence of increasing competence. The evidence can take many forms. Each piece of evidence should ultimately be approved explicitly as ‘satisfactory’ by the supervisor.

The form of both the progress file and the summary are agreed by supervisors and student. The probation assessors simply confirm that the progress file has been maintained and the summary has been provided.
