Does every child matter? A qualitative study of the phenomenon of street children in Bulawayo, Zimbabwe

  • Favourite
  • 1194 views

Ian Ndlovu, 10:30 am to 11:00 am

Cloud created by:

SocialLearn
8 June 2010

This study is about life experiences of street children in Bulawayo, Zimbabwe. The broad aim of the study is to explore and examine life experiences of street children and to capture their own perspective of life in the streets of Bulawayo presented in their own words. The study departs from what Hecht (1998) calls an obsession of many writers whose focus is on causes and what can be done to remove them from the streets.

Children living on and working on the streets in Bulawayo and other cities is a post-independence phenomenon which has over the three decades reached alarming rates for the residents, local authorities and central government. It is not unique to Bulawayo but a common feature in many cities in Zimbabwe and in many developed and developing countries around the world.

A lot has been said and written about Zimbabwe’s political and economic problems with very little mention of Zimbabwe’s children and more so street children. Street children are vulnerable and constitute what can be termed ‘Zimbabwe’s Forgotten Children’.

Studies on street have been descriptive which has been unhelpful in improving practice and policy formulation. Methods used in many studies have surveys and interviews focusing on the life on the streets and reasons for being on the streets.

The study will employ a number qualitative methods for example  ethnographic narratives (generative), semi-structured interviews, participant observations, mental and depot maps and photographs I will also discuss ethical issues such as gaining access and informed consent.

Street children are a global phenomena. Today, in the news, the London mayor was talking about the homeless of which street children is part of that group. Most of the models of street children has been from Latin America. The image of street children is that they’re prone to be criminals and they’re exploited. His aims are:

  • To develop and understanding of life experience of street children
  • To examine how they negotiate spaces and places that they occupy (The street is a public and private space at the same time. In the Western world there is a structure of home, school and play. But street children are different – but their access is negotiated i.e. places where they won’t be chased away)
  • Understand how children construct and contest their identities (such as social exclusion – what is important to the children)
  • Explore development of policy responses

He is talking about a task force that is defunct now in Zimbabwe and that there are only 2 NGOs that helping with the welfare of children. There is hope with the unity govt’ that things will move on.

There is no common definition of street children. Most common one is by UNICEF – where boys and girls use the street as their home and a source of livelihood. This model is based on Latin America. This model has been transported to Africa and wonders if you can do this since there are differences in culture and languages. In Latin America, it has been colonised with mostly people speaking with one language. In Africa, there were many colonisations, with different religions and different languages.

There is a wide definition since there are children from slums, children who are abandoned, children who live with their families on the street etc (?)

There is very little literature on Zimbabwean street children [removed][removed] – the major one by the UN looked at the number of the children on the streets. Another is on the child-headed households.

After the UN rights of the children charter, Africa developed their own since they felt it didn’t reflect their cultures etc, but it has not been put into practice.

There was an operation clean-up where street children were removed from the streets.

Wakatama (2007) did a project on street children looking at the children’s rights – how they’re implementing the UN charter.

His methodology would be grounded throy via a child-centred method in which he hopes to use ethnographic methods. He wants the children to tell him their life stories from as far back as when they remember – these would be narratives. He wants also them to do drawings – since drawings help draw out memories.

He hopes to get access by through gatekeepers such as working as a volunteer or work in a drop-in centre. He hopes to get informed consent from the children and signed as well. For his safety he hopes to use the drop-in centre for the interviews.

He is showing an image of Bulwayo and some street children – they look desolate ... and lonely. “I’m a dog, I’m a dog” – quotation from a S .Africa’s street child – as a dog lives outside and gets scraps.
