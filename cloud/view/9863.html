You do the math!

Title You do the math!

Narrator

Support a pre-reg. nursing student with medication calculations who had failed an online test (a 100% pass is needed)

Situation

Use of OU Live as students is on a distance Learning Programme: date, time and place suggested by student.  I am the student’s tutor and we have met face-to-face once – however I used to be her teacher in FE so knew her when she was 16 and taught her then for 16 years.  Student has to achieve 100% in the calculations or will fail the programme: she is exceptionally anxious and I have been given one hour of support time. Therefore there were pressures of time, of situation (not face to face) and outcome! 

I has guided student that prior to meeting she review key aspects of calculations (being able to divide and multiply decimals and convert fractions to decimals.  Key online resources were highlighted.

Task

Both of us were aware of the types of questions which student has got wrong in the previous test and a ‘back to basics’ approach was felt to be best by students as she panics when given a ‘test’. She is also very aware that patient safety is paramount and it is a no fail test. Knowing the student I felt that it was best to show her what she can do and what she does know (rather than more questions merely highlighting the opposite).  Via Application Sharing we visited basic numeracy skills site (BBC skilswise) and reviewed calculations.  I shared my ‘top tips’: fail safe methods of checking answers to drug calculations and the use of mnemonics to help remember the formula needed to work out correct dosages.  We kept it simple (in fact most ward-based drug calculations will be simple and relatively straightforward – they just have to be right.  We reviewed how to do a ‘rough estimate’ first – by knowing ‘roughly’ how many tablets it must be it will also add as a ‘checker’ if the answer you get deviates from these too much (if that makes sense). 

Actions

We chatted through her feelings about the test (embarrassment and a degree of humiliation). This was not unexpected.

Use of OU Live to help the student was not as ‘good’ as working together I felt as the IT was slow and ‘clunky’ so slowed us down. I indicated that I could request more time if needed (and it was).

Via Application Sharing we visited basic numeracy skills site (BBC skilswise) and reviewed calculations. There was a balance to be struck between not wishing to ‘expose’ the student to what she didn’t know and also identifying key areas for further input.  All students are aware of patient safety comes first.

 I shared my ‘top tips’: fail safe methods of checking answers to drug calculations and the use of mnemonics to help remember the formula needed to work out correct dosages.  We kept it simple (in fact most ward-based drug calculations will be simple and relatively straightforward – they just have to be right) and I also did this in part for the student to gain confidence

 We reviewed how to do a ‘rough estimate’ first – by knowing ‘roughly’ how many tablets it must be it will also add as a ‘checker’ if the answer you get deviates from these too much (if that makes sense).  We worked through some calculations together and I gave some ‘homework’ for her to review with her mentor in her workplace.

Results

Overall I felt that the use of OU Live for this was not ideal – this was more related to the emotional aspect of the session (not sure how to explain this really – but I sensed that the student felt ‘awkward’).  The student does not use a video and does not use a microphone – this hampered the session so I followed it up with a telephone call.

The student was able to complete some simple calculations and has additional resources to progress and develop.  There was something lacking though and I have requested a further session which I will consider carefully how to manage and consider something more meaningful

Reflections

Reflecting upon this situation there is no doubt that there are some very helpful resources: interactive sites where you can work out injections etc.  I will direct the student to use those but there was a ‘tension’ – possibly because of what is at stake if the student is not successful.  This situation will happen again and in the future I will discuss further with the student ahead of the actual session
