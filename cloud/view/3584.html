Dear colleagues,

I am writing to you about an ongoing initiative about the use of web comics in schools.

With the advent of web technology, web comic books have appeared as a new, promising, instructional medium. Web Comics is a plurimedia medium that combines text and imagery as well as hypermedia and streaming elements. Web comics can be interactive means for studying as well as for self-assessment. Children having read some pages of the web comic book, could be asked to respond to an online assessment test, e.g. a test that has the form of unfinished dialogues among the characters. Web comics can offer scaffolding to difficult scientific disciplines and concepts.

It is believed that web comics can be an effective instructional medium especially for reluctant readers and poor students. Their use in education is based on the Clark’s & Paivio’s (1991) dual coding theory, which supports the importance of imagery and narration in cognitive operations.


We are interested in your opinion about the use of web comic books as an instructional medium that can give children the chance to use material from their own lives to conceive, write, and illustrate stories.

We are working on the Educomics  project [http://www.educomics.org/] Educomics  is a European Union Comenius education project under the LifeLong Learning Program.  It is building sets of resource materials for teachers based on research and piloting in EU schools, so that teachers will have clear examples and guides to use digital comics in their teaching practice.

The EduComic project places children in the role of creators of web comics, rather than merely readers of educational comics. Using special web comics authoring tools, like ComicLab, children write and draw comics using their imagination, creativity and personal experiences and interests. This is supposed to be a highly engaging and motivating learning process.

The point of the project is not to drop a web comic book on a child’s desk and say: “read this.” Rather, the project aims to train teachers in order that they design learning scenarios where students (in groups or individually) will have the opportunity to draw upon stories (often complex) that they then revise, publish and share with their peers in a learning community.

It would be nice to hear if you believe in the use of web comics in schools. In case you have experience in using web comics in classroom, please send us your findings.

This is an ongoing work which seems to have quite good acceptance from the school communities in 5 EU countries (Greece, Cyprus, Italy, Spain, UK).

Looking forward to hearing your opinions,

Simos
