Introducing social learning

Introduction by Chris High (OSRG) at the Social Learning Symposium

Cloud created by:

Chris High
12 March 2010

Some starting assumptions about social learning:

  • It's a common term in lots of fields (domains if you like)
  • It is understood differently in these fields, at least on the surface
  • It relates to a diverse range of practices

The purpose of the symposium is to explore the different understandings and practices relating to social learning amongst researchers at the Open University, drawing particularly on OSRG and the SocialLearn team.

The most general idea I know of for social learning is that it is a space of platform which supports collective exploration of an idea or situation.  I'd like to propose this as an heuristic idea to drive discussion by inviting anyone taking part to express their concept and experience of social learning in these terms.

So for example, the work I'm doing this weekend is writing up the use of participatory video as an evaluation methodology in community development, where the purpose of the evaluation was considered to be to produce social learning rather than to extract information relative to judgements about the achievement of programme goals.

In this mapping, participatory video - the act of bringing people together to make a film about themselves - is the 'space' which enabled collective exploration of a community development group's work.  

Where it goes beyond the heuristic model above is that I'd also want to include something about the purpose or output of the platform in terms of what it enabled - different ideas, different capacities and different relationships.  

Comment 2 by SocialLearn

SocialLearn
7:14pm 13 March 2010 Permalink


We've raised the question on the SocialLearn blog of what social learning actually is, and whether it is more than a subset of social constructivism.

We're interested in the ways in which Web 2.0 extends the possibilities for social learning, making it possible not only to locate and access a vast amount of content from all around the world, but also to engage in extended interaction around and about this material. Learners – particularly those learning outside formal settings – may find themselves adrift in an ocean of information, struggling to solve ill-structured problems, with little clear idea of how to solve them, or how to recognise when they have solved them. We think it’s here that social learning has its place – helping people to use these resources to construct knowledge together effectively.

We're currently working on the assumption that social learning can take place when people:

• clarify their intention – learning rather than browsing

• ground their learning – by defining their question or problem

• engage in focused conversations – increasing their understanding of the available resources.

These three actions help us to build meaningful connections online, and offer learners the benefits of co-operative activity and of collaboration.

Comment 3 by Martin Reynolds

A couple of definitions that I find useful.

1.  John Friedman (American urban planner) describes social learning as the third of four traditions informing planning  - the other three being ‘social reform’, ‘policy analysis’ and ‘social mobilization’ {Friedman, 1987) . He contrasts social learning with the more control-oriented tradition of policy analysis which, he claims:

"is a form of anticipatory decision-making, a cognitive process that uses technical reason to explore and evaluate possible courses of action.... Social learning, on the other hand, begins and ends with action, that is, with purposeful activity… It is the essential wisdom of the social learning tradition that practice and learning are construed as correlative processes, so that one process necessarily implies the other." (Friedman, 1987 p. 181)

 

2. Niels Röling (dutch environmentalist): SL is  “a change from multiple to collective and distributive cognition” (Röling, 2002)

Röling describes SL as an alternative device for resolving natural resource dilemmas;  distinguished from conventional command-and-control models involving  (i) regulatory measures  (ii) fiscal (dis)incentives and (iii) instruction through transfer-of-information.  Social learning in contrast is an action-oriented process modelled on a constructivist view of knowledge aimed at transforming a situation.
