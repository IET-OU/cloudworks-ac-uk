Developing internships and work based learning opportunities for mature students: creating opportunities and raising aspirations

  • Favourite
  • 1104 views

Cloud created by:

John Rose-Adams
27 September 2012

10.00am to 3.00pm, 31 October 2012

The Open University Milton Keynes

The seminar will provide an opportunity to consider the particular needs of mature students when universities attempt to develop employability supporting opportunities through internships and work based learning modules. The seminar will consider the difficulties in developing employability opportunities for those who may already be working in low paid employment and for those with non-traditional educational histories.

Further details are available from The Higher Education Academy site

There is no charge to attend the seminar, but a place must be reserved.

To book on, click the 'book onto event' button on this page, if you experience any difficulties with the online booking procedure please email thematic.seminar@heacademy.ac.uk. 50 places are available on first-come, first-served basis.

The seminar will also be broadcast online with opportunities throughout the day to engage and contribute to the discussions. To receive further details and register to attend virtually, please emaildawn.patton@open.ac.uk.

The seminar will focus on:

  • internships in the third sector as providing opportunities for career changers
  • work based learning modules within the field of working with young people as support for career development
  • the notion of maximising opportunity and raising aspirations for mature students
  • the issue of developing employability skills through working experiences for those from non-traditional educational backgrounds.

The event will be of value to university policy makers and practitioners interested in developing a collaborative approach to the provision of internships in smaller organisations, those supporting employability for mature students in a range of disciplines, and those supporting the employability and social mobility of students from a diversity of backgrounds.

The event will be simultaneously broadcast via the Open University’s webcast platform with opportunities for virtual engagement throughout the day.

Aims/outcomes

  • To consider the issues for mature students as ‘career changers’ and the way in which collaborative university mechanisms can support work experiences in third sector organisations
  • To consider the issues for mature students as ‘career developers’ and the way in which work based learning modules can support opportunities for social mobility and learning in the workplace.
  • To discuss the issue of developing employability skills through working experiences for those from non-traditional educational backgrounds.
  • To consider how internships and work based learning can offer opportunities and raise aspirations of mature students.


Information about sessions and speakers

Career development and work based learning: real lives, imagined futures

Roger Harrison, Tyrrell Golding and Stephen Harrison, The Open University

This presentation will draw on data from a small qualitative study eliciting stories of progression towards professional status from students on a youth work programme. The students were selected from each of the three levels of the Open University’s professional qualification in Youth Work. In seeking to elicit biographical narratives the study is able to make connections between the personal, social and occupational factors which shape the hopes, fears and performance of these students. In examining these narratives of progression towards professional status we hope to gain some insights into the challenges faced by those designing and delivering work based programmes of study for mature students in higher education.

The presentation will:

  • Establish the nature of the professional field in which this programme is located, acknowledging that journeys to professional status differ between occupational groupings.
  • Identify the nature of the programme these students are pursuing – work based learning within a distance teaching format and conforming to a specific regulatory framework.
  • Examine the narratives of progression which have emerged from the research and the kind of questions these raise for educators.

Internships, University Collaboration, and Career Change Opportunities

Dr Martha Caddell, Third Sector Internships Scotland / The Open University in Scotland and Fiona Boyle, Third Sector Internships Scotland

With record levels of graduate unemployment, renewed focus is being directed to the role of volunteering and internships as mechanisms for enhancing employability, gaining work experience, and securing routes into permanent jobs. There has been widespread recognition that a ‘degree is not enough’ and work relevant skills and experience are crucial in a highly competitive job market. Yet there is also concern about the potential elitism of (often unpaid) internship and work placement schemes, where access to opportunities are based on who you know or your ability to work without pay rather than on merit. Much debate has focused on the implications for young people seeking to enter the graduate labour market, yet there are clearly parallel concerns for mature students wishing to return to work after a career break or change career.

This session offers space to discuss this increased focus on internships and work experience and the particular challenges faced by mature students? Can internships offer meaningful opportunities to enhance skills and open opportunities for career change or development? What are the practical challenges facing students, universities and employers in developing placements and internships that will genuinely help enhance student employability?

In highlighting the emerging issues, we will draw on our experience developing and implementing Third Sector Internships Scotland (www.3rdsectorintern.org.uk). This is a collaborative programme developed by Scottish universities and the Scottish Council for Voluntary Organisations. Focusing in particular on how mature students have engaged with the programme, the discussion will explore key issues including:

  • Mature students motivations in applying for internships, and their experience on and post internship.
  • Making internships meaningful for employers and students.
  • The challenges of working across multiple universities and with small and micro organisations.
  • The practicalities of getting an internship scheme up and running.

Fiona Boyle is Co-Director of Third Sector Internships Scotland. She has particular responsibilities for partnerships and learning. Fiona is based at Queen Margaret University. Her background is employability and skills development, having previously held the post of Employability Coordinator at QMU.

Dr Martha Caddell is Learning and Teaching Coordinator at the Open University in Scotland, with particular responsibility for employability and teaching and learning enhancement. She is also Co-Director of Third Sector Internships Scotland and is responsible for research and evaluation activity on the programme. Her background is in education and skills development, particularly in international and comparative contexts.

Tyrrell Golding is a Lecturer in Education Youth Work, in the Faculty of Education and Language Studies.

Roger Harrison is Senior Lecturer in the Department of Education, in the Faculty of Education and Language Studies. He is currently core member of the team developing the Foundation Degree in Working with Young People. His research interests focus on lifelong learning, with particular emphasis on issues of policy, pedagogy and identity. His recent publications and conference papers on social theories of learning; informal learning; learning for professional development; learning and identity.

Stephen Harrison is a Lecturer in Education Youth Work, in the Faculty of Education and Language Studies.

Brenda Little is an experienced Educational Analyst with detailed knowledge of the UK higher education (HE) sector spanning the past 20-30 years, and of HE systems in mainland Europe. Her expertise includes developing, undertaking and managing policy-related higher education research in relation to the student experience, graduate employment and work-based learning.
