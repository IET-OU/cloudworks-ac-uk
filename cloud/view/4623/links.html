What Works and What Doesn't?

Cloud created by:

Emma Johnstone
26 September 2010

This is an area I have a number of years of experience in, yet, frequently, I still find certain behaviour challenging to manage.

Not counting the practicalities of other stakeholders, pupils and priorities in anyone's working day!

Extra content

As a gymnastics coach I am responsible for the safety of my gymnasts.  Most educators are in this position, to some degree.  With one gymnast with ADHD currently struggling to maintain his behaviour and concentration for the length of a training session I need to find means of keeping him safe in the gym.  

At times, his training hours have been cut to a number that he can manage.  This works, but it isn't ideal.  As he gets older, he will be expected to maintain his behaviour in life, work and family situations etc.  As a talented gymnast he would benefit from being able to access the training available to him.  

My current plan - testing in progress! - is The Gymnastics Challenge.  It can be reworked to suit any context.  

I have typed up a list of appropriate tasks he could do when his attention wanes and staying on-task is not going to happen.  Some of them he will consider boring i.e. 'Do 50 sit-ups.'  Others are intended to be fun, 'Dance on the spot for one minute.' or an opportunity to be kind, 'Think of something nice to say to another gymnast.  Tell them.'  Some will be a bit of a challenge 'Hold a handstand for 30 seconds.  If you can do this aim for one minute!'  These are cut up, laminated and put in a bag to be rummaged and selected blind one at a time, as needed.  The task selected must then be performed.  

In this way he will hopefully have a safe 'break' when needed (at an appropriate point in the session i.e. not during warm-up) while remaining safely directed.  

The vicariousness of receiving a desired or undesired task is likely to appeal to the risk-taking behaviour generally performed more by those with ADHD than without.  

The opportunity to be kind, or present a pleasant aspect of the self to others, is valuable to the self-esteem of those, especially children, who are more likely to be viewed as troublesome or annoying by others.  Some less understanding adults can contribute to this as much as other youths.
