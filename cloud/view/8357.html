Title   Using technology to arouse interest in a research topic

 

Narrator

As module leader  for a first year undergraduate ‘Research Skills’ course with a Business degree,  I was required to assign a topic to students from which they could devise their own research questions for the semester,  carrying out initially secondary, then some basic primary research.

Situation

 Sessions were programmed for a two-hour seminar, once a week for 12 weeks,  and took place in one of the IT teaching rooms.   Class sizes were small (approx. 10 students),  with a highly international profile. There were 16 computer terminals arranged in rows of 4-5.  Students’ prior experience of essay, report-writing or research would have been very much dictated by prescriptive research titles

Task

The challenge was to create ‘buy in’ for the essentially arbitrary research topic of ‘productivity’.  With limited time on the course, it was important that students were not only able to generate this interest,  but that they started to work in small groups to devise their own  research questions.    The task was therefore designed to stimulate early engagement in the topic,  highlight some of the possible areas for more focussed research questions, and to create an inclusive environment for those students who would normally shy away from group discussions.

Actions

The seminar started with eliciting definitions of ‘productivity’ from students, how it might be measured and how it might be characterised differently according to job/activity.    This was followed by a viewing of a 10 minute TED talk by Jason Fried:  ‘Why work doesn’t happen at work?  (www.youtube.com/watch?v=5XD2kNopsUs)   Students were told their recollection of the content would be tested at the end.

At the end of the viewing students were invited to open an Inspiration mind-map template which I’d previously created  around the main points of the talk and were invited to fill in as much of the detail as possible (see figure 1)

Finally, students were invited to open participate in a live forum online with other members of the seminar group:

1. ‘Write a brief post (max. 100 words) on one aspect of Jason Fried's talk on productivity.    It may be something you  agree or disagree with,  or something you feel he has overlooked or over-simplified.   State why you feel this way.

2. When you have finished, find a post by another class member which you disagree with and respond to that.’

 

 

 Results

 

In the elicitation stage,  students seemed engaged and were forthcoming with their definitions of ‘productivity’ so that by the time the video was shown they seemed engaged with the theme.   Jason Fried’s talk is accessible, dynamic and situates productivity in the context of the everyday office and so the task of filling in the mind-map template (post-viewing) was also carried out with some energy.

For the live forum discussion,  I had left a period of 20 minutes,  and again there was a fairly high level of activity and urgency within the room with approximately 35 posts made by 10 students, although with varying degrees of insight and frequency (see figure 2)

 Reflections

Student engagement

With hindsight,  I would like to have extended the live forum activity,  since students seemed a little reluctant to finish within the allotted time;  this may have been some indicator of student engagement with the task.

All students were actively engaged with annotating the Inspiration mind-map.  I find it hard to envisage the same level of participation among some of the same students,  who are usually reluctant to take notes,  around  a paper-based activity.

 Inclusivity

This was my first attempt at using a synchronous forum within class time;  some of the more articulate,  usually domineering students initially seemed frustrated at not being able to express their views orally,  but then were amongst the most prolific posters.  

As an exercise in inclusivity, I was interested to monitor the quality of contributions from the more retiring students;   one,  who was virtually silent in class posted the joint second highest number of responses,  making salient points.   Another posted a fairly non-critical synopsis of the talk (see figure 3)

 Figure 3 - student post

However, because I was actively involved in posting on the forum myself, it did afford the opportunity of acknowledging posts from these students.

Developments

If carrying out the task again,  I would consider trying to structure/pre-format the threads a little more, given the limited time and to focus the discussions more thematically.

 Any comments on further developments or perceived weaknesses gratefully received!
