This question came up over cups of tea yesterday when we were "debriefing" with Grainne, James Clay and colleagues about points raised in her and Peter's presentations on Tuesday morning.  We laughed when remembering the spontaneous trickle of applause when Peter said that "for 10 years we've been talking about flexible learning... how has our teaching practice changed?" - something to that effect. I wondered aloud what would have happened if he'd asked for a show of hands at that point, of people who felt they really were teaching in fundamentally different/new ways, grappling with "new pedagogies"? What do these "new pedagogies" look like and how we could characterise them? Images of "mobile learning" (in all 4 guises as outlined by James Clay in his keynote) come to mind - but when those learners are learning "anywhere anytime", making choices about place and time of access, or when the in/formal learning divides blur, or when learners and teachers interact in virtual worlds, (etc etc) what's new about the learning/teaching PROCESS and related strategies ? Are "new pedagogies" at the level of delivery and facilitation or deeper than that (e.g. assessment, curriculum and course design?) Or in other dimensions altogether?

Comment 3 by Lindsay Jordan

Lindsay Jordan
11:36am 9 December 2009 (Edited 12:55pm 9 December 2009) Permalink


At last year's Plymouth e-learning Conference Mark Russell and I facilitated a workshop that explored the concept of an e-pedagogy. The workshop aimed to explore what features of learning environments are needed to promote and support learning (see a range of responses at http://lindsayjordan.edublogs.org/what-promotes-learning/) and to establish whether these features have any uniqueness or specific relevance to technology-rich or technology-sparse learning environments.

I would suggest that - as Gilly Salmon identifies in her 5-step model - when a teacher or facilitator is introducing a tool or technology that requires new skills - new ways of thinking or working, the introduction and embedding of these tools becomes a crucial part of the teaching and learning process. Some may see this as a revision to traditional pedagogies. However, the process of learning to use these tools in a constructive way is optimised by the same factors as any other learning process; prompt feedback, reciprocity, active learning, etc., and 'learning how to learn' - active study techniques, teamworking skills - made an appearance on curricula long before the rise of Web 2.0.

Some may view the increasing importance of group communication and collaborative working as a shift in the nature of the learning process, and therefore representing a shift in pedagogy, but the importance of contact, reciprocity and co-operation in learning has been widely accepted for decades. The tools we use now are more efficient, accessible and mobile than the blackboards, flipcharts and typewriters of yesteryear - which is fabulous. Other than this - does 'good learning' look any different? Personally I doubt it, but I'm open to other suggestions :-)

Hi

Are "new pedagogies" at the level of delivery and facilitation or deeper than that (e.g. assessment, curriculum and course design?) Or in other dimensions altogether?

To me the problem I'm experiencing-or at least at national level, ie, Spain- is that new pedagogies are indeed taking place at the delivery and facilitation level (depending on teachers/institutions awareness and drive for change) but they clash with 'old' pedagogies at the deeper level as you call them. So to me, right now, it should be extremely important to work on the new pedagogies at that deeper level. I've seen, read, heard quite often colleagues asking how to assess a whole course where students work is via blogs, wikis, etc...and students complain about the fact of new pedagogies taking place at the delivery level but then the old model of exam and reward applies, at all stages (primary, secondary, college, uni)...

There's still a lot to be changed (and synced bw what governments say and the bills they pass or don't pass on education, basically they should lead by example: change assessment methods at national level if you are recommending teachers to innovate at the delivery & facilitation level!)

Comment 6 by Lindsay Jordan

Lindsay Jordan
3:04pm 10 December 2009 Permalink


Claire - thanks for the feedback on the blog - watch this space ;-)

I'd be interested to know what you think caused your son to respond in that way (about SL)... there are a lot of conversations going on at the moment about the long-standing backlash against the new ways that people work, play and learn; the constant rumblings that these new tools and methods are causing a 'dumbing down' of society; increased emphasis on the trivial, a lowering of standards and a loss of traditional, valuable skills.

I'm sure this backlash against technology existed long before the Ancient Egyptians discovered the use of rollers and pulleys to build the Pyramids. 

Peter Marris (Loss & Change; 1974) explains beautifully how individuals and society respond to change, and how important these conservative impulses are to the change process. These ever-present rumblings may seem like ignorant, damaging nonsense to innovators and evangelists, but should not be ignored; they fuel the rigorous examination of the nature of and reasons for change.

If your son is questioning what value new technologies like Second Life might have for his own learning, then you have an interesting and productive conversation on your hands. He may equally be accepting unquestioningly something he has read or heard that took a more conservative view about virtual worlds in education... but he is only 13 so there is no need to panic - independent critical analysis is probably a couple of years away yet ;-)

We had Carl Weiman speak to us at Auckland University of Technology on 15 December 2009 about a new scientific approach to science education (see his initiative at http://www.cwsei.ubc.ca/ and his Optimized University piece at http://bit.ly/CarlWeimanThinkPiece).
