The Student Voice

  • Favourite
  • 627 views

Cloud created by:

Kylie Matthews
11 March 2016

Date: Tuesday 12 April 2016

Time: 10:00 - 12:30

Venue: Library Seminar Rooms 1&2

In 2015 the Student Engagement Project devised the following definition:

'The Open University apires to engage all our students, individually and collectively, as partners in the assurance and enhancement of their educational experience. Student engagement at the University is defiined as the range of ways in which students are active participants in shaping their educational experience.' (SEC-2016-01-06)

This event focuses on our aspirations and our current practices in engaging with the Student voice.

We will explore examples of feedback and partnership approaches on the spectrum of student engagement: from University-driven (via forums, surveys and VLE data collection) to student-driven (from ad hoc emails/tweets, to positions of potentially significant influence, such as partnerships with OU module teams).

We will hear about Student Consultation Forums from Art and Law showing how these have been used to impact module design. Bart Rienties will then present the extensive work being done analysing the Student voice as communicated via VLE data drawn from 151 modules. Finally, we hold an open discussion on the aspirations, challenges and questions we have around involving students as partners.

The event aims to:

• Raise awareness of different feedback and partnership approaches available
• Share good practice in how to make use of these
• Stimulate us to consider how we ensure the feedback loop is successfully closed and feedback providers made aware of how their input was used.

Programme

  • 10.00-10.30 - Student Engagement: Shaping Educational Experience - Jerard Bretts (Assistant Director, Student & Staff Engagement, Academic Services)

Jerard will set the scene with a brief overview of 'student engagement' and what it means and then focus on the work of the OU's Student Engagement Project, which is identifying distinct areas for engagement in feedback and communication; curriculum and learning design; and new modules such as a student partners scheme.

  • 10.30-11.00 - Student Consultation Forums - Rachel Garnham (Academic Services), Emma-Jayne Graham (Arts), Elizabeth Mullett (Law)

A brief overview of the student consultative process, its opportunities and challenges and some examples from Arts and Law of how students engage with online forums and the impact this can have. Event Slides

  • 11.00-11.20 - Coffee/Tea
  • 11.20-12.00 - The impact of learning design on student behaviour, satisfaction and performance: a cross-institutional comparison across 151 modules - Bart Rienties (IET)

Pedagogically informed designs of learning are increasingly of interest to researchers in blended and online learning, as learning design is shown to have an impact on student behaviour and outcomes. Although learning design is widely studied, often these studies are individual courses or programmes and few empirical studies have connected learning designs of a substantial number of courses with learning behaviour. In this study we linked 151 modules and 111.256 students with students’ behaviour. Event Slides

  • 12:00-12:30 - Case study and open discussion: Students as partners in module production – Should we be doing it? Or are we just blindly following a fashionable trend?- David Vince (TEL), Elizabeth Ellis (TEL), Ingrid Nix (FHSC)

Introduced with a brief case study of a Staff-Student Hack Day to generate ideas about curriculum with 50:50 split (students: staff).
Facilitated discussion on:
• What are the imagined benefits and values?
• What OU examples, if any, do we already have?
• If we want to make it work, how should we go about it?
