Barriers to Web 2.0 transfer, Charles Crook, University of Nottingham (UK)

  • Favourite

Cloud created by:

John Cook
18 November 2011

It is often claimed that young peoples’ recreational engagement with the ‘participatory web’ should be a strong force to encourage the appropriation of web2.0 tools into the classroom. Some findings from a project on engagement with web2.0 by secondary school students will be outlined. This research suggests that while the central dispositions associated with using web2.0 services are certainly apparent within out-of-school activities, the nature of how those dispositions are configured in recreational contexts contrasts with how they are invoked within classroom contexts. This suggests a need for more careful consideration as to how to achieve the possible continuities between outside-school and inside-school internet experience.

Issues on continuity and access between school and home (everyday life)

  • Pupils own PCs at home, but a little more have to share PCs at home whith others
  • asking for various computer, media and Web2.0 activities during the last 24hours
  • asking which of those activities took place in school, at the intersection, or at home

Asking kids for desired stress of activities / which Web2.0 activities should be focussed in class

Shift along to following themes which are dominating the conversation around digital media (so they are not exclusive):

  • collaboration: Knowing and knowledge is increasingly considered to be collaborative.
  • inquiry
  • publication: Opportunities to create audiences for this knowing and knowledge
  • literacy: Cultuvating expressiveness beyong text

Asking students how they perceive

  • collaboration: potential redundancy / inefficiency, not knowing how to deal with collaborativeness
  • inquiry: Uncertainty of how to aggregate information
  • publication: visibility, evaluation, permanence
  • literacy: legitimate forms of expressiveness or perception as being recreational represenational acivity

Synthesis:

Mapping inside scool activities against outside school activities and thus revealing tensions along the categories collaborative, inquiry, publication, literacy.

  • Movements? Lets work on those lines of continuity and let's consider the kids practices of personal media use.
