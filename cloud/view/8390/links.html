Title Coaching instrumentalists in Military Bands

 

Narrator

Tutor responsible for introducing the idea of coaching to members of the bands.

 

Situation

I was requested to give a 1 hour presentation about coaching instrumentalists in Military Bands. The audience was a whole group of experienced musicians, some of these being leaders in their respective instrumental sections. The audience was assembled in a lecture theatre type hall for a whole set of lectures and activities linked to their work within bands. I was presented as the teacher, ‘authority’.  They expected a PowerPoint presentation comprising set of facts that would allow them to coach younger musicians.  This expectation stems from the militaristic culture to learning that they are used to.

 

Task

I was trying to get them to think about the concept of learning and support initiated by a provocative act, gain some experience and insight by means of a practical session, as well as offer some personal experience.  According to the feedback received, my alternative approach was well received.

Actions

Initial act: Icebreaker

I asked them to pronounce my surname ‘Czepiel’; after several incorrect attempts, put up ‘Czech Republic’ then asked some others the same request. After hearing the first – and correct – answer, I asked them whether I had taught them anything.  

The group was divided equally into those who thought I had taught them something, and those who felt otherwise.  The group were challenged by un-militaristic approach, asking their views and not spoon-feeding them information. This seemed to interest them and initiated quite open and interesting comments and discussions throughout the session.

Main part: Coaching Scenario.

 I had a real band musician who had agreed to be a guinea-pig learner and asked individuals in the group to come and act the role of a music coach.  We worked our way through about ten minutes work of an actual coaching session, which was interpolated by a ‘pause in the action’ to reflect on what had just happened and to critically evaluate the action of the person coaching, and what others would have done instead. 

The primary issue I tried to emphasise was to put the learner at the centre of the activity, the focus for comment. This I needed to repeat several times when asking for comments on the actions observed.

 A few of the group seemed to dominate discussion and answers in particular.  This I addressed directly by saying ‘can someone else answer the question...?’ This worked even though there were some slight pauses, or I had to actually ask individuals to respond. 

Concluding summary.

I summed up some of the more important points raised in the session, stressing the fundamental idea of putting the musician/learner at the centre of the activity and suggesting coachers should not overstep their capabilities. 

Results

Certainly the learners were engaged with the activity.

 I expected there to be those who dominated the discussion, and some more passive learners. 

I id not expect the learners to have such a strong set of viewpoints and heated discussions to take place between the. 

I was surprised they were glad not to have a PowerPoint presentation.

List the expected and unexpected outcomes of your actions. To what degree did you meet your objectives? What additional outcomes did you engender? Provide evidence to back your claims.

Reflections

Certainly an older and more experienced individuals can have strong views. Be prepared to cope with these and views contrary to my own.

Many of the things I wanted them to appreciate was articulated by members of the group. By given them some ownership of the learning, they engaged much more. 

I was surprised by the range of reactions to the session, ranging from quite ‘surface’ approach by a few individuals to much more ‘deep’, open and inquiring approaches from others.
