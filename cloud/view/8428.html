Location: Library seminar Rooms 1 & 2

What to do with UGC, how to support and whether to encourage and use/reuse it are issues which every module and teaching related project will encounter, whether intentionally or not.  Forum messages, blog posts and wiki entries are some of the more obvious and familiar examples of UGC within OU teaching. The scope of UGC extends from video and photo-sharing to social networking exchanges (tweets, likes, ratings and reviews), part of a massive expansion in network traffic initiated and controlled by students. Whether generated formally as part of directed activity or informally and serendipitously, the quality and diversity of this content is challenging in terms of pedagogy, management and ethical/legal issues.  This event will draw on a recent University-wide scoping activity and experienced users of UGC will present mini-cases from across the OU and encourage discussion of an issue which is evident in all our online teaching.

The recording of the event will  be made available on Stadium.

Extracts

User generated content – what is it all about? (Chris Pegler)

Chris will set the scene by reviewing the many forms of user-generated content, drawing on examples from within and outside the OU. This approach is one which is particularly effective for project-based courses where students are generating artefacts (e.g. in H818 The Networked Professional, new in Autumn 2013), but it is an approach which has been used effectively (although perhaps described as something else) since the OU started. She will make a case that as more students and tutors have access to the means to create and share resources and we explore open education in earnest within and beyond the institution user generated content is revived, reinvigorated and everywhere we look! 

Headlines from the Scoping Study (Richard Nurse)

During 2012 Library Services carried out a short piece of consultancy to investigate issues and challenges around User Generated Content.  This study interviewed a number of stakeholders around the University to look at how UGC  was being used at the Open University and looked at best practice from elsewhere.  Headlines and recommendations from the study will be outlined during the presentation.

Challenges of UGC (Liz Mallett)

Liz will be exploring the challenges of UGC from different perspectives: students/users, information management, and curriculum development/scholarship

iSpot (Jonathan Silvertown)

Knowing the correct name of an organism is the key to learning about it, to sharing your observations with others and to contributing to the corpus of scientific knowledge. Un-named species are effectively invisible and impossible to conserve. Although putting names to species is fundamental to biodiversity science, conservation and education, it is a skill that is largely absent from formal biological education at all levels. The OU social networking website www.iSpot.org.uk  is designed to remedy this. Our 25,000 users share their observations and get help with identifying what they've seen, building up reputation as they learn and make good identifications with the help of experts from more than 90 natural history societies. All the content in iSpot is user-generated and as the number of observations increases towards a quarter of a million records, the uses of these data for education and science multiply.

B716 Online Residential School (Kristen Reid)

Using a mix of wikis, and synchronous and asynchronous discussions, students attending the Online School of the MBA Stage 1 module: B716 Perspectives and Practice participate in a role play related to a real life case study.  This brief presentation will demonstrate how traditional pedagogical approaches such as case studies, group presentations and role plays are transformed into online activities that allow students to create and share material in successful and engaging ways.
