Technological challenges and opportunities of 3 generations of distance education pedagogies

Terry Anderson

 

 

Values - we can and must continuously improve the quality, effectiveness, appeal, cost and time efficiency of the learning experience

Student control and freedom is integral to 21st C lifelong education and learning

Continuous education opportunity is a basic right

 

Wendy Dexler - University of Florida 7th grade learning today video 

DE/online/blended paradigm wars

 

Three technology generations of DE

  1.  Correspondence
  2.  Mass media 0 video, TV
  3.  Internet, interactive

 

Learning as dance - Anderson, 2008 technology sets the beat and the timing, pedagogy defines the moves. Each technology affords different things for different learners.

 

Social construction of technology - interpretative flexibility, relevant socail groups, design flexibility, probkes and conflict

Bijker, W 1999 of bicycles

 

  1.  Behaviourist/cognitive - self-pasce, individual study
  2.  Constructivist - groups
  3.  Connectiviist - networks and collectives 

 

 

1. Tell em what your going to tell em, tell em and then tell em what you told em Direction Instruction

 

Gagne’s events of instruction, 1965 Basis of instructional systems design ISD

 

Cognitive revolution - chunking, cognitive load, working memory, multiple reps, split -attention effect, SORDEN, 2005, variability effect, multimedia effect

 

Logically coherent, largely context free, capable of being transmitted, readily defined through LOs

 

New developments - reflection amplifiers, social indicators, multiple representations, student modeling and adaptation - analytics

 

Adaptivity in ubiquitous learning - extensive modelling of learner’s actions etc, real time monitoring of learners

 

Open student models  Susan Bull et al 2007

 

Learner analytics - mining information about patterns of behaviour in order to extract useful information about learning that can then be applied to improve education

 

Open content and resources

 

Issues in distance education series open press books aupress.ca and IRRODL.ORG

 

Designers log - case studies in instructional design Michael Power

 

Are you more than your content? Lack of motivation for DE content developers to use OER? Many DE developers and faculty define themselves by the production of quality content not by the consumption and customisation of it

 

Large course teams 

 

Thinking cap tool for coordinating content as it is developed

 

Phil Ice enahncing teacher presence through voice annotation of essay and project assignments - increased impact of feedback, students appreciate voice, increase amount of feedback, saves time, using adobe acrobat

Ice, Curtis, Phillips and Wells, 2007 JALN, 11(2), 3-25

 

Many ways that technologies enhance production and generation of 1st gen pedagogies

 

  1. social constructivist pedagogy

 

new knowledge builds on prior knowledge, importance of context, errors important, learning active, importance of language, negotiation/meta-cognition and evaluation, multiple perspectives, need for knowledge to be subject to validation and application in real world context

 

Honebeing, 1996

Jonassen 1991

Kanuka and Anderson 1999

 

Greenhow, Robelia 

 

Based on group learning - motivation, feedback and alternative/conflicting viewpoints

 

Srpinger, Stanne and Donovan 1999 pg 42 value of groups

 

Johnson and Johnson 1989 cooperation and competition - theory and research

 

 

New tools - collaborative tools (doc creation, management, versioning, timelines, calendars, strong notification), security trust, decision making/project management tools and syn/asyn conversations

 

Perera 2009 TRAC system user model and adaptation for groups

 

SNAPP moodle analytics Wollongong Social dynamics of the group

 

Gestures Avatar Kinect Body language rich human presence tools

 

Asyn voice technoloigies Voicethread

 

Problems with groups: restrictions in time, space pace and relationship, overly confined by leader expectation and institutional curriculum control, usually isolated from the authentic world of practice, low tolerance of internal differences (Cousin and Deepwell, 2005), pathological politeness, group think (Baron, 2005), poor preparation for lifelong learning beyond the course

 

  1. Connectivist

 

Learning is building networks of information, contacts and resources that are applied to real problems

 

Siemens, 2004 six principles of connectivism

 

includes learning may reside in non-human appliances

 

Its emergent, distributed, chaotic, fragmented, non sequential, and contextualised

 

Networks add diversity to learning Ronald Burt Brockerage and closure 2005

 

 

Kays and Sims 2006: 411 key for creativity

Pettanati M 2007

 

Connectivist Learning Design - Connection forming/selection/filter, contribution and involvement, reflection/metacognition and awareness/receptivity cycle

 

Special issue of IRRODL Connectivism March 2011

 

Transparencey, persistence leaving trails through the net Clay Shirky 2008: 162

Stuart Kaufman - adjacent possibilities

 

Brown and Duguid 2001 Networks of Practice

 

distributed , shared common interest, self organising, open, no expectation of meeting or knowing  people in the network, social capital building, increase expo ruse to the adjacent possible

 

Nahapiet and Ghoshal 1998 how to build - need to be motivated, structural support, cognitive skills, social connections/reciprocity

 

Challenges: privacy, control, dealing with disruptive change, institutional support, sustaining motivation and commitment

 

ELGG provides a more open environment

 

Leveraging the collective Dron and Anderson, 2011 group, network, and set

 

Bloom and Anderson, Krathwohl et al 2001

 

Creating, evaluating, analysisng, applying, understanding and remembering

 

Dron 2011

1st gen hard but scalable

2nd softer, 

3rd v soft, emergent, forces more learner control

 

Laland anf Galef 2008  Humans are uniquely social
