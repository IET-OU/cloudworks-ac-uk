From the website:

As the school year begins, be ready to hear pundits fretting once again about how kids today can't write—and technology is to blame. Facebook encourages narcissistic blabbering, video and PowerPoint have replaced carefully crafted essays, and texting has dehydrated language into "bleak, bald, sad shorthand" (as University College of London English professor John Sutherland has moaned). An age of illiteracy is at hand, right?

Andrea Lunsford isn't so sure. Lunsford is a professor of writing and rhetoric at Stanford University, where she has organized a mammoth project called the Stanford Study of Writing to scrutinize college students' prose. From 2001 to 2006, she collected 14,672 student writing samples—everything from in-class assignments, formal essays, and journal entries to emails, blog posts, and chat sessions. Her conclusions are stirring.

The Educause Horizon report 2008 suggests that while web-based tools are rapidly becoming standard in education and in the workplace and technologically-mediated communication is the norm, fluency in information, visual, and technological literacy is not formally taught to most students. Beetham et al (2009, see link in the right hand column) provide similar insights.

In the light of this we need new and expanded definitions of  literacy, or indeed the presence of literacies which are based on mastering underlying concepts of knowledge,  information, navigation, negotiation, collective or distributed intelligence.

The white paper Confronting the Challenges of Participatory Culture: Media Education for the 21st Century (Jenkins et al., 2006) that emerged from the Project on New Media Literacies (see first link on the right) identifies the three core challenges: the participation gap, the transparency problem and the ethics challenge, and shares a provisionary list of skills - of which I list above - needed for full engagement in today's participatory culture. Still though, participatory culture invokes mostly informal learning in non-mainstream and largerly autonomous spaces, revealing - amongst themselves and when 'transferred' in formal educational contexts - many of the tensions outlined above in the excerpts from the Wired article. And here're the questions:

  • What are the methods for teaching and evaluating critical and novel forms of literacies  at all levels of education?
  • what is the role of the family and other peer groups for learning with and through technologies and digital media?
