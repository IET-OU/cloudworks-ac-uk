Tackling childhood obesity. Evaluating physical activity in children

  • Favourite
  • 1545 views

Adam Lockwood, 10:00 am to 10:30 am

Cloud created by:

SocialLearn
8 June 2010

Obesity and overweight have increased significantly since the mid 1980’s in children in England, however recent data suggests a slowing of the trend. Obesity and overweight are now recognised as public health issues however their classification, treatment and prevention are still under considerable debate. A decline in physical activity levels and an increase in sedentary behaviours have been implicated as one of the determinants for the rise of obesity and overweight in recent times, however research has revealed that these behaviours are partly influenced by the obesogenic environment in which we live.

Community based physical activity interventions may be effective in preventing or treating overweight and obesity in children. Chances4change is a portfolio of 62 community based projects located in the South East funded by the Big Lottery Fund Well-being programme. Individual projects within chances4change address physical activity levels in children. Evaluation of these projects is vital to display their importance and effectiveness. However, at this stage little or no evaluation of physical activity behaviours has been conducted amongst the chances 4change projects due to various difficulties.  Therefore I have the role of designing and developing a toolkit for use with these projects to investigate the effectiveness of community based physical activity interventions.

This presentation will discuss the key insights and questions I have found through my literature review so far. I will also discuss any difficulties I may come across when evaluating physical activity in children within the chances4change projects. This will form my rationale for where my research will continue.

Key Challenges for evaluation

  • Will levels of physical activity in children increase because of the projects?
  • Can we measure a child’s change in motivation for taking part in physical activity?
  • Will change in fitness take place because of the influence of the projects and how do we measure a change in fitness? For example is there baseline data available such as BMI?
  • How to do the evaluations such as using questionnaires etc.

 

What is obesity?

He says it is a controversial topic. There is considerable debate on its classification and even the treatment and prevention in terms of whether we use diet, exercise, individual or environment. WHO classified it as a disease in 1985, but Gard and Wright (2005) that it is not a disease in itself but a risk factor for other diseases.

Why a problem in children? It has increased by 30% from the mid 1980s. This picture is quite complex, as it is rather disproportionate in the social gradient – higher in the lower quartile (?). Further there are differences between, black, minority and ethnic groups.

Obesity in children is associated with a clustering of risk factors for cardiovascular diseases and associated with an increase in Type 2 diabetes. There is also negative self image and low self-esteem. The cost of obesity is nearly £7 billion in 2002 (direct and indirect costs).
