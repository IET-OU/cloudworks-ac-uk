Title: Teaching Agile Development

  • Favourite
  • 803 views

Cloud created by:

Marija Cubric
23 June 2011

Author: Katie Taylor

Abstract: UCLan began teaching Agile development in 1996 replacing a second year undergraduate module based on SSADM with one based on DSDM.  Initial setup involved a considerable overhead in terms of resources, facilities and materials.  Association with a local practitioner group was an invaluable source of knowledge and support.

We experienced different reactions from different types of students;

  • with and without work experience
  • with a more technical and with a more human orientation
  • more or less relaxed in difficult situations

Teaching all these different people in the same classroom led us to value a ‘safe’ learning environment.

For students without much work experience, teaching from the book is particularly unhelpful. There are two sides to ‘Agile’. 

  • The tools and techniques such as Pair Programming or Test Driven Development, that are no different to teach than any other new tool or technique
  • The philosophy and culture that underpins the approach.  Students with little industrial or life experience badly need an example so that they can apply the ideas as they learn.

Our response was to let students gain first-hand experience of being part of a development team.  We ran and recorded role-play of a first workshop based on a Health Centre case study.  Students watched the recording and then documented requirements, issues and observations of the meeting process.  They continued from where the recording left off, role-playing and recording subsequent workshops.  Module assessment included students’ analysis of the processes they observed within their workshop.  Module feedback from students stated that they disliked the process at the time but by the end of the module they consistently rated it as the best thing about the module.  

We have now built up a series of recorded role-play by students, lecturers and practitioners. A script is essential because it is difficult even for home students with good hearing to make out everything that is said. We believe that teaching through experience of workshops gives practical experience in the classroom and reflects the agile approach to valuing individuals and interactions over processes and tools.

Hi Katie & All,

Thanks for outlining your approach to Agile Software Development in your abstract and the various challenges you faced especially when trying to square-the-circle of experienced professionals versus novice developers as one naturally encounters within any student cohort i.e. 'straight-from-college' students versus 'some years experienced' students. I face an almost identical set of challenges as you do in explaining the criticality of software engineering and Agile methods within that versus other possible approaches, most notably the SSADM (or 'waterfall') approach. I intuitively felt the resource issue would a serious constraint in teaching this method and you confirm this here; I think Ian Sommerville's exercises are helpful in this regard where he deals with Agile methods, from a book-based approach but true 'animation' of the whole process is only really possible, in the 'classroom' at least, by role-play with appropriate organisation and a suitable case-study. I use previous experience of working at BAE Systems, Warton & BT to attempt to contrast the various software development approaches, including Agile methods, with limited effect even when using Airbus videos and software occurrences on civil aircraft as case studies.

Would be nice to follow-up with you in respect of the extensive experience you have in this area.

Best wishes,

Rob Manderson
