Problem-based learning spanning real and virtual words: a case study in Second Life

  • Favourite
  • 1285 views

Notes on a paper by Judith Good, Katherine Howland, Liz Thackray (University of Sussex, Sussex Innovation Centre)

Cloud created by:

Juliette Culver
20 January 2009

Case study investigating use of Second Life by students taking a course on interactive learning environments where students had to create innovative learning experiences for clients.

 

Background on educational uses of Second Life

 

  • About 36 universities have a SL presence
  • Used in Multimedia Design Course at Heriot-Watt
  • Used with architecture students at the University of the West of England
  • 'Maths Island' at University of Warwick
  • Virtual Maths Support Centre at Coventry University
  • Vibrant educational community e.g. SLED mailing list
  • But many SL educational experiences replicate traditional teching e.g. presentations/lectures in SL
  • But e.g. SL can support physical simulations with interaction between multiple learners and the simulation
  • SL also offers strong social component.
  • Its immersive nature allows role-play e.g. with social care encounters or medical diagnosis skills. Easier to suspend disbelief.

 

 Case Study

 

  • Focus of study on how the use of SL in Problem-Based Learning context changes nature of learning and teaching experience.
  • Teaching team (paper's authors). Three people: 1) Overall running and facilitation 2) SL expert 3) Technology facilitation and preparing and running sessions
  • Students worked in project teams, paired with clients from Sussex Learning Network partner institutions to design learning experiences for them.
  • Clients asked to identify issues difficult to teach in real life, hold initial meeting with team and to provide additional input as required during the project. Also invited to presentations and their feedback welcomed.
  • Member of teaching team available to liaise with clients throughout the project.
  • Subjects were well outside expertise of the students.
  • Assessment in terms of a portfolio of work: the learning experience, a machinima showing highlights of their learning experiences, group document describing project and development process, and an individual document providing reflection, critique etc.
  • 41 students took part in 8 teams
  • Description of research and consent form at first course meeting
  • Following first client meeting, teams produced initial spec and were given feedback from the teaching team
  • Teams provided weekly updates in the form of SL notecards with feedback provided
  • Members of teaching team frequently present in SL throughout course and available for discussion in RL and SL
  • Supported by 8 2-hour sessions introducing students to learning theories, SL etc.
  • Studio-based approach

 

Data collection

  • Assessed student work main source of data (including student reflection)
  • Weekly notecards
  • E-mail comments
  • Group project workspaces
  • Standard end of course assessment form
  • Informal interviews with students both in RL and SL
  • Course team communications
  • Feedback from clients

 

Theoretical underpinnings and contribution of SL to PBL

  • Based on constructionist pedagogy
  • But used PBL approach too
  • SL allows tutor role to be blurred (not SL expert)
  • Awareness of tutor presence in SL - easy availability for feedback
  • Also subtle changes in type of tutor-student communication
  • Buzz around SL makes it 'relevant' to students and generated lots of interest in the students' work
  • Cutting-edge environment, so no solutions already known to many problems!
  • Difficult aspects: knowing what the students were actually doing (e.g. deathly silence), students building classrooms - but later moved to doing things only possible in a virtual environment
  • SL good for problems where limited resources etc. in real life would make them impossible, makes it easier for students to take ownership. Safe environment for experimentation.
  • Strong online community supports student independence
  • Work in SL easily viewed and appreciated (esp via machinima) but maybe issues with land or technical issues

Contact details: ALT-J, Volume 16, Issue 3 September 2008 , pages 163 - 172
