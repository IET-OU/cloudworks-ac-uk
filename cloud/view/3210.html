Hi All,

I am the one responsible for that 1.3MB file - sorry! I am also off to a late start since Cloudworks does not like my usual email address - an important lesson for online learning I think.

Following on from contributions already made, I think a phased approach is needed. We need to focus first on courses with large student numbers but low pass rates and throughput and then, as Paul has suggested, get some idea from students about the kinds of additional support they feel they need. This was a central aspect of the previous ODL Student Support Task Team proposal so it is a pity that the project approved by the STLSC to 'experiment' with 2 modules has been shelved.

We also need to look at the larger context: graduation rates in higher education in RSA are pretty poor generally - in both contact and DE modes. I cannot believe that all institutions teach badly and therefore conclude that many students entering HE are underprepared - not suprising considering how badly RSA fares in comparative literacy and numeracy at school level. I was hoping that Unisa's ROAP project would shed some light on this issue ... but suspect that we could achieve a lot simply by providing more intensive integrated academic literacy development in our first level modules - and such support could be mediated by more advanced students, community leaders, teachers etc rather than discipline specialists.

The issue of extended study time was explored by a CHE task team in 2007 at the request of a previous Minister and concluded that rather than extending the time, HEIs should simply teach better! And a key indicator for that was creating opportunities for more active student engagement with content in context. And THAT raises the issue of greater decentralisation as the historical and current model of tutor support at Unisa has been centred on student request after registration for tutorials at one of the regional centres. So we also need to be able to keep tabs on just WHERE students are and WHO they are. Saide has consistently argued that a learning centre is a place where learning happens and that could be a virtual space for those who have access and inclination but also workplaces, schools, community and church halls etc. In such a scenario Unisa's regional centres would then be support hubs for a much wider network of physical community centres and virtual spaces. I would see this increasingly being coordinated at a programme level rather than centrally.

Hi Roger et al - Unisa servers were down this whole weekend (...) so that is why it was so quiet!

A number of things are happenging at present:

1. Unisa is seriously engaging in (on the one hand) raising its academic admission requirements for its degrees and diplomas based on the reality that (i) we have the lowest (the minumum) admission requirements in the country; (ii) our students are totally under-prepared for higher education (iii) and we are under-prepared for such type of students. Therefore it would seem as if, in the absence of a thorough, application screening, the language requirement for the language of tuition will be raised substantially - students between 50-60% will be allowed to reqister for only four modules a year while students with 60% or more will be unrestricted. The "restricted students" will be needing extra student support. This is the thinking at this stage - not yet formalised.

2. While this seems like "closing" the gates of higher education, Unisa is also in the process of formulising alternative pathways for students who are impacted by these new requirements, like a Higher Certificate which will be a qualification in its own right, but which will provide a "pool" for preparing under-prepared students with potential.

3. It would seem as if the four year degree is back on the table on the national agenda. This will further allow additional academic support and preparation in students' first year.

From where I sit (or run...), I think the three institutional factors impacting the most on increasing our student success is the lack of a formalised student profiling on application (which will allow placing students on a range of trajectories) and the linking of effective and appropriate institutional interventions based on the evidence of this profiling. The first of these is in development phase and will provide Unisa with actionable intelligence, while the latter is less coordinated (at present) but there is a task team that will put a strategy on the table. The third factor is a 16 week tuition cycle in a country where postal services are unreliable, students' postal addresses are often those of family members and employers, students internet connectivity is costly (and often erratic) and students, who are underprepared, register for too many courses per semester.

Apologies for this long response - but I suspect it maybe adds value to thinking about the impact of tutors taken the above. If we don't address the above, tutoring will be less-effective.

Paul
